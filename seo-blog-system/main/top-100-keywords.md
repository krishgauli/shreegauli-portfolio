# Top 100 Keywords

## How I Built This List (And Why Most Keyword Lists Fail)

I want to be upfront about something: putting together a list of 100 keywords feels a bit like overpromising. Most keyword lists end up gathering dust because they're disconnected from what you'd actually write about. So I tried to approach this differently.

The goal here wasn't to chase volume. It was to find phrases that real people — small business owners, startup founders, maybe an overworked marketing manager — are actually typing into Google when they're trying to solve a problem. The kind of thing where, if we wrote something genuinely helpful about it, we'd deserve to rank.

A few things shaped my thinking:

First, I leaned heavily toward low-to-medium difficulty targets. This is a consultant site, not HubSpot. We don't have a domain rating of 90. So I looked for long-tail queries where a well-written, specific piece of content can realistically show up on page one within a few months. Most keywords here sit in the KD 0–35 range, though I slipped a few medium-high ones into the long-term bucket because they're too relevant to ignore.

Second, I tried to match every keyword to a real content idea — not just a title, but something that would actually be useful to read. If I couldn't picture a post that a person would genuinely benefit from, I dropped the keyword. There's no point ranking for something if the content behind it is hollow.

Third, there's a local thread running through this. Dallas, small business, startups — those modifiers show up because they narrow the competition and they match the audience. But honestly, most of the keywords work nationally too. The local stuff is more of a bonus.

One thing I'll admit: volume estimates are rough. I pulled ranges from a mix of tools and cross-referenced trends, but these aren't gospel numbers. Search volume fluctuates, tools disagree with each other, and the real metric that matters is whether the content converts — not whether 800 or 1,200 people searched for it last month. I'd rather rank #3 for a 200-volume keyword that brings in strategy call bookings than #1 for a 2,000-volume keyword that attracts the wrong audience.

The list is split into three tiers. The top tier is what I'd start writing about this week — things where we already have a published post to optimize, or where a new piece of content could realistically rank within two to three months. The medium tier needs a bit more effort, maybe creating pillar content first or building some internal linking. And the long-term tier is for when we've built enough authority that Google considers us a credible voice on broader topics.

One more thought before the tables: please don't treat this as a keyword-stuffing checklist. Google's helpful content guidance is pretty clear — they reward people-first content, not scaled low-value SEO tactics. Every keyword here should prompt the question, "Can we write something about this that's genuinely better than what's already ranking?" If the answer is no, skip it, or at least wait until we have something real to say.

## A Final Note on Using This List

This list is a starting point, not a production schedule. Before writing anything for a keyword, take five minutes to search for it yourself. Look at what's ranking. Ask whether you can honestly do better — or at least bring a perspective those results are missing. If you can't, move on to the next one. There are 100 keywords here. You don't need all of them to work.
